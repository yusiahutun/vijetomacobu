"The cause of death and method of death is unknown pending receipt and evaluation of investigation data relating to Ms. Jones' abduction and death," the pathologist concluded.

Turner stated that the drug concentrations observed in Jones' skeletal muscle could not be utilized to quantify intoxication or toxicity. It could only indicate that the medicines were present in the body.

"The drug concentrations recorded by the toxicology laboratory have no clinical relation since postmortem skeletal muscle, unlike blood, is not a clinically proven and validated matrix for interpretation," Turner's study stated.

"As a result, interpreting drug concentrations in postmortem skeletal muscle for intoxication or toxicity is scientifically and medically unjustified."

The pathologist stated that she discovered a linear skull fracture and left knee contusions. She said that because of the body's skeletonization (stage of decomposition), she couldn't tell whether the injuries occurred before or after Jones died.

"Microscopic inspection shows the existence of left knee contusions," Turner stated. "The fracture of the calvarium's inner table (cranium) may or may not signify antemortem (before death) damage."

Its existence, together with the circumstances surrounding Ms. Jones' disappearance and the environment in which her remains were recovered, strengthens the possibility of murder as the cause of death."
