Exfoliation is an extremely important step in skincare. And, when it comes to tough calluses, corns, cracked skin, dry cuticles, and dead skin buildup at the feet, it is important to opt for a more robust form of physical exfoliation. This compelled me to look into the best pumice stones for feet, as it has been an age-old practice to use them in one’s skincare routine, and I wanted to explore more about them.

Pumice stones are excellent keratolytic tools for scuffing away tough layers of dead skin in the stratum corneum, thereby buffing the skin and smoothing its texture (study by JAAD). Due to their porous nature, these rocks work extremely well when used with water and soap, as they help form a rich lather and scrape away stubborn impurities and stains off the skin. Several dermatological studies have used pumice stones extensively, in addition to chemical exfoliants like salicylic acid to effectively treat skin issues such as corns and warts (research published on NCBI). 

Besides, having inspected several types of callus removers in the past, such as stainless steel scrapers with horrifying, cheese-grater-like appearances, I deem pumice stones as a relatively gentle, safe, yet efficacious tool for exfoliating the feet. My list of recommendations below comprises some of the choicest products that offer varying levels of abrasion to suit different needs. I hope you like them!

    How to Select a Good-quality Pumice Stone for Your Feet?

    Consider the Texture

    Pumice stones are either coarse-textured, comprising large to medium-sized pores, or smooth-textured, comprising medium to small pores. While the former is ideal for abrading calluses, corns, and cracked and extremely dry skin, the latter helps with gentler exfoliation and buffing. Also, if you want to prevent calluses before they start to form properly, a smooth-textured pumice stone is preferable as it wouldn’t cut or abrade the skin more than it calls for. Some synthetic pumice rocks are double-textured, which can be used for both abrading and polishing in a more elaborate foot care routine.

    Consider the Source/Materials of the Stone

    Ideally, a pumice stone is of natural origin, formed out of volcanic lava. They are environmentally friendly and very durable, suitable to last for many years. However, as they are natural rocks, they are very abrasive in nature and are only suitable for severely dry, dead skin cells and tough calluses, as rubbing them on those areas won’t cause any pain. 

    Now, synthetic foot files, mimicking the same texture of natural pumice stones are becoming common these days. The best feature about synthetic ones is that they may comprise both hard and soft textures, catering to different skin needs. Some manmade pumice stones may still be composed of natural ingredients other than volcanic lava, such as the Gilden Tree Natural Terra Cotta Pumice Stone, which is made with terra cotta. 

    Many artificial pumice stones work well with soaps and cleansers, to enhance the cleaning process. However, these products may or may not be good for the environment. So, depending on your specific needs, priorities, and complete knowledge about how the product was sourced/manufactured, you can choose to invest in one.

    Consider the Construction/Design

    Volcanic pumice stones are often naturally shaped like an egg or a sphere, and fit in an average-sized palm. However, some modern products use the finely crushed grit of pumice rocks and bake it evenly on the surface of a handled foot file, to enhance its functions and convenience. In the case of artificial pumice stones, they may consist of rounded, rectangular, cylindrical, and other miscellaneous designs. You should always invest in one that is easy to hold, grip, and maneuver and does not fatigue your hand.

    How to Use a Pumice Stone?

    Step 1: Start by soaking your feet with warm, soapy water for about 5 to 10 minutes. 

    Step 2: This step is optional, but if you have a callus-remover gel handy, use it and leave the product on your skin for 3 to 5 minutes. Then, rinse it off. 

    Step 3: Pat your feet dry with a soft and clean towel.

    Step 4: Start by scrubbing the pumice stone in gentle, circular motions on the callused, cracked, or dried-out regions on your feet.  

    Step 5: If the stone has a smoother side to it, use it to buff the softer areas on your skin. If it doesn’t have a smoother side, use the same coarse-textured stone, but massage it gently on softer skin areas. Be sure to not put too much pressure on it.

    Step 6: Rinse the skin, and pat dry. Follow up with a rich moisturizer or salve.

    Tips for Prolonging the Life of a Pumice Stone:

    1. Cleansing

    Pumice stones easily accumulate dead skin particles, bacteria, grime, germs, and other impurities after every use. Hence, it is important to scrape that out by scrubbing it with a bristled brush dipped into antibacterial soap. When rinsing it, ensure you get rid of any soap residue. Then, infuse the pumice stone in water mixed with an antiseptic solution to kill germs and prevent the outgrowth of bacteria. You can also choose to sterilize the pumice after every use.

    2. Drying

    It is very important to dry the pumice stone completely before storage, as any wet particles of the stone stored for a prolonged period can start to crack or flake. After rinsing, wrap the pumice in a super-absorbent towel and leave it there for 2-3 minutes. Then, place it on a clean, dry surface exposed to plenty of sunlight, and allow the pumice to air-dry.

    3. Proper Storage

    Unlike what you may have seen in some stock images on the internet, it is a bad idea to stack pumice stones on top of each other. This can cause the stones to chip or crack. Furthermore, avoid storing pumice stones in small cases or airtight containers, as the friction can cause them to damage. Instead, place them in a high-quality, sturdy, clean, and spacious container with a lid on it to ensure longevity. Also, store the pumices in a moderately cool, dry place and avoid exposing them to extreme temperatures.

    4. Ventilation

    It is also important to store the pumice in a highly ventilated space to prevent it from sweltering, as that would smooth out its edges and reduce its exfoliation prowess.

    FAQ's

    What are some hygiene protocols to follow when using a pumice stone?

    Yes, there are several. After exfoliation, it is extremely important to cleanse and disinfect the stone with plenty of water and potent antibacterial/antiseptic solutions. Following this, it is important to air-dry the stone under natural sunlight, to ensure it is completely dry before storing.

    How often should a pumice stone be replaced?

    As the shelf-life of an average pumice stone is less, it is ideal to replace it every month. However, if your pumice is particularly durable, without bearing much impact on its exfoliation properties, its usage can be prolonged for another two weeks.

    How often should I use a pumice stone on my feet?

    Not more than 2-3 times a week.

    Is it safe to use a pumice stone on sensitive skin or diabetic feet?

    It is best to avoid using pumice stones on diabetic feet. Also, if you have sensitive skin, limit the pumice exfoliation to hardened regions of your feet only, such as calluses, corns, dry cuticles, and dead skin layers.
