Delaware man, 28, executed by lethal injection over the shooting of ex-girlfriend's lover

Death before dishonor': Last words of man executed for shooting ex-girlfriend's lover... and he didn't even want a final meal

By Associated Press and Daily Mail Reporter

A Delaware man was executed Friday for killing his wife's lover.

Shannon Johnson, 28, waived his right to further his appeal and sought to speed his death by legal injection.

Johnson was convicted of the murder of Cameron Hamlin, 25, who he suspected of having an affair with his ex-girlfriend and mother of his child.

Lethal injection: Shannon Johnson was executed on Friday for the 2006 murder of Cameron Hamlin who was dating his former girlfriend and the mother of his child

The name of the woman has not been released.

Johnson shot Mr Hamlin, an aspiring musician, in September 2006 when he found him sitting in a car with his former girlfriend, years after they had separated.

Later, Johnson shot the woman but she survived.

According to delawareonline.com, Johnson evaded detection between the two shootings by dressing in a Muslim woman’s all-covering burka.

Dressed in the garment, with only his eyes exposed, he fired and hit the woman, smashed the window of her car and pulled her out.

Prosecutors said that the only reason she survived is because his gun jammed and Johnson fled the scene. 

The felon’s last meal was chicken lo mein, carrots, cake, wheat bread and iced tea - the same as the other prisoners; he had no special request.

Victim's family: Jasmine Hamlin, sister of Cameron, said that Johnson appeared 'most conscious' about what was happening and seemed 'nervous' and 'scared'

According to a Department of Correction spokesman, Johnson spent his last days sleeping, eating, reading, watching television, writing letters and speaking with his attorney.

Johnson was already strapped to a gurney when witness were led into the execution chamber.

'Loyalty is important. Without loyalty you have nothing. Death before dishonour,’ he said when the prison warden asked if he any final words.

Johnson then uttered a few words in Arabic before he closed his eyes and the first of three chemicals began flowing through his veins.

As the sedative pentobarbital was administered, Johnson's breathing became laboured and his chest heaved several times.

A few seconds later, he was motionless and showed no more signs of movement. The entire process took less than 15 minutes.

Mr Hamlin’s family were present and spoke after the execution.

Begin healing: The victim's father, Vandrick Hamlin, said that it had been a long road since his son was 'brutally murdered' but the family can now start to recover

'Now it's time for us to focus on putting our lives back together,’ said his father, Vandrick Hamlin.

His sister, Jasmine Hamlin, described the state of mind of the felon in the minutes before his death.

'He was the most conscious and [he knew] what was taking place. He was nervous and I felt like he was a little scared. I think he realised… what life really means,’ she said.

Several appeals were made on behalf of Johnson by his attorneys after he decided not to pursue any further appeals when the state Supreme Court upheld his death sentence in 2009.

Federal public defenders twice sought to intervene in the case without Johnson's consent. 

Even up to days before his death, his attorneys sought to save him.

In a filing submitted last week, they sought permission from U.S. Chief District Judge Gregory Sleet to allow Johnson's estranged sister, Lakeisha Truitt, to argue on his behalf that he was mentally retarded and ineligible for the death penalty.

However, following a lengthy hearing last year, Superior Court Judge M. Jane Brady declared in February that Johnson was mentally competent to waive his right to further appeals and that he understood the legal consequences of that decision.

The federal appeals court said it was 'firmly convinced’ that the Superior Court's finding of Johnson's competency was entitled to a presumption of correctness, and that Sleet had abused his discretion in granting a stay of execution.
