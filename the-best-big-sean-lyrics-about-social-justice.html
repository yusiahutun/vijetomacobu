The best Big Sean lyrics about Social justice

Social justice is a prevalent theme that resonates with many artists across various genres, and Big Sean is no exception. Known for his introspective lyrics and thought-provoking messages, the Detroit-born rapper has frequently touched on social justice topics throughout his career. Through his music, Big Sean addresses issues such as systemic racism, inequality, and the struggle for justice, offering listeners a powerful lens into the realities of marginalized communities. In this article, we will explore this important theme in his lyrics and cite examples where social justice is evident.One of the most profound songs where Big Sean tackles social justice is "One Man Can Change the World" from his album "Dark Sky Paradise." This track, which features Kanye West and John Legend, serves as a heartfelt tribute to his late grandmother. The song provides a profound perspective on the power of individual actions to bring about systemic change and highlights the importance of social justice. Lyrics such as "They used to tell me, ‘Don't forget where you come from’ / That's why I rap this way, reminiscing on our younger days" exhibit his determination to uplift his community and inspire others to do the same.Another track that reflects Big Sean's commitment to social justice is "Bigger Than Me" from his album "I Decided." In this song, he delves into the broader societal issues that affect marginalized communities. He addresses systemic problems, stating "All this foolishness been goin' on way too long / You goin' to work, tryna pay the bills / Bills, bills, bills, you can't pay no more." These lyrics draw attention to the struggles faced by working-class individuals, highlighting the inequality and financial strain perpetuated by societal structures.Furthermore, on the song "Light" from his album "Detroit 2," Big Sean collaborates with artists like Jeremih and Ty Dolla $ign to amplify the message of social justice. This track emphasizes the importance of inner peace, resilience, and empowerment in the face of adversity. By doing so, Big Sean encourages listeners to rise above challenges and fight for justice for themselves and their communities.In addition to these songs, Big Sean touches on the theme of social justice in his track "No More Interviews." In this song, he turns to introspection and recounts the pressures he endures as an artist and a Black man in America. He raps, "I guess it took me a little bit longer than I thought it would to get here / But, hey, I know exactly where I'm goin'." These lyrics highlight the perseverance required to navigate a society that often marginalizes voices like his, encouraging his listeners to stay steadfast in their own journeys and aspirations for justice.Another remarkable example of Big Sean's exploration of social justice can be found in the song "Deep Reverence" from his album "Detroit 2." In this track, he collaborates with Nipsey Hussle, and together they examine the impact of gun violence on marginalized communities. Big Sean's lyrics "F*** rap, I'm a street legend / Black love me with a deep reverence" clearly embody his fight for justice and recognition within the music industry while addressing the larger struggle for equality faced by Black individuals.Lastly, "One Man Can Change the World," "Bigger Than Me," "Light," "No More Interviews," and "Deep Reverence" are just a few examples of Big Sean's commitment to social justice. These songs demonstrate his efforts to raise awareness about the issues plaguing marginalized communities, while inspiring listeners to take action and strive for change. Through his introspective lyrics, Big Sean embodies the essence of a socially conscious artist who uses his platform to address the urgent need for social justice.
