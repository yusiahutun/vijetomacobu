Fossil of 'mythical Chinese Dragon' uncovered as experts find new remains

The remains of a 'mythical Chinese Dragon' have been discovered for the first time.

Palaeontologists have now pieced together the fossil of the 240-million-year-old marine reptile, known as Dinocephalosaurus orientalis. The remnants were discovered in Guizhou Province, southern China.

The preserved remains come from the Triassic period of China and measure a whopping five metres (16ft). The beast had an extraordinarily long neck, with 32 separate neck vertebrae, boffins revealed.

The reconstructed specimen is based on seven specimens. These include five newly discovered remains.

This is the first time the Dragon's appearance has been seen in full. The reptile was first identified in 2003 but there had never been a more complete specimen, including one which is fully articulated, until now.

Dr Nick Fraser, Keeper of Natural Sciences at National Museums Scotland, said in a statement: "This discovery allows us to see this remarkable long-necked animal in full for the very first time. It is yet one more example of the weird and wonderful world of the Triassic that continues to baffle palaeontologists."

He added: "We are certain that it will capture imaginations across the globe due to its striking appearance, reminiscent of the long and snake-like, mythical Chinese Dragon."
