People are only just realising what the colours mean on a darts board

Darts is a simple game to play – but there are plenty of questions around the sport itself.

Plenty of people wonder why each leg starts on 501 – we've answered that here. Others are curious why the bullseye is worth 50 points when it's at the centre of the board – we've answered that one as well.

But what about the different colours on the board itself? A common dartboard, such as one that will be used in the Paddy Power World Darts Championship final between Luke Littler and Luke Humphries, consists of four colours.

Those colours are black, white, red and green. Assorted double and treble sections are green and red, as are the bullseye (red) and the outer bull (green). Single number sections are split between white and black.

But why is that? Well, the origins of the board's colour scheme can be traced back to its early design. Darts boards in the present day are made from sisal fibre, which are the strands from a cactus.

Way back when, boards were made from the end grain from tree trunks, the growths within which lent themselves to the segmented design we still see today. Gradually, more colour was introduced to make them more appealing and to enhance the experience.

It is believed that the red and green colours – the most striking on the board – were originally intended as a nod to British history and culture. Red is associated with the Tudor rose, while green is related to the House of Lancaster.

Plus, while there's historical context to the colours on the board, they also allow players to focus. The Treble 20 segment is one of the most important on the board, and the one that players will aim for most.

The fact it is coloured in red makes it easier for players to focus on as they throw their dart. Littler and Humphries are expected to hit plenty of Treble 20s – and 180s – when they clash on the Alexandra Palace stage on Wednesday night (January 3).
